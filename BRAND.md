## Positioning statement

For friend groups traveling together, TripUp is the app that takes on the responsible parts of the
trip, so the group gets to just be on the trip. It turns spontaneous choices and shared costs into
fast, fair, visible outcomes — where group chats bury decisions in scroll and spreadsheets handle the
math but ignore the group's momentum.

## Audience

Primary demographic: adults roughly mid-20s to early 40s, digitally fluent, financially comfortable
but not indifferent to cost — the kind of person who already runs their life through subscription
apps. Mainstream, mobile-first, comfortable paying for tools that remove friction; not a niche or
student-first audience.

Accessibility and inclusive design are a baseline requirement regardless of the primary skew. This
demographic is a center of gravity for tone and humor calibration, **not** a gate on who the product
serves.

**The Organizer.** The most proactive member of the group — sets up the trip, invites others, keeps
things moving. Wants the app to make them look competent without extra effort; low tolerance for
anything that adds steps. Voice for this persona: assume competence, confident and efficient, never
over-explain.

**The Participants.** Want to be involved without being overwhelmed — suggest ideas, vote, stay
informed. Lower investment in the app, higher sensitivity to friction; more than a couple taps and
they're back in the group chat. Voice for this persona: quick and low-friction, respects that they
didn't ask to think hard about this right now.

Humor and cultural references skew to the mid-20s to early-40s range — current enough to land, not so
current it ages out or excludes people outside that band. Avoid humor that assumes a specific income
bracket, nationality, or relationship status. The reference scenario is intentionally generic (a
group trip to Lisbon); the tone should be too.

## Brand character

Decisive. Fair. Quick-witted. Steady under chaos. Straight-shooting. Built to get the group back
outside.

Think of the friend who somehow ends up being both the one with the plan and the one everyone
actually likes — not the loudest voice in the chat, the one whose word people trust without a second
thought.

The clearest way to say it: TripUp is the group's digital travel assistant. Not an agent that books
things for you — an assistant that makes it easy for the group to decide things together and stay
square on money. It takes on the responsible parts (deciding, tracking, settling) so everyone else
gets to actually be on vacation. Not a voting tool, not an expense tracker — that's just what it does.
What it *is* is the one member of the group who's got the logistics handled so nobody else has to
think about them.

The point of all that speed and fairness isn't efficiency for its own sake. Every minute spent
debating dinner or untangling a tab is a minute not spent on the trip. TripUp exists so the money and
the logistics stop being the thing the group is doing, and go back to being background noise to the
actual adventure.

## Naming note

"Trip up" is an idiom for stumbling, for making a mistake. The brand leans into that irony on purpose:
TripUp exists so the group never trips up on logistics or money. The wordplay stays at the name level
— tone should carry the brand on its own without leaning on puns, unless a moment in the experience
calls for one naturally.

## Tone of voice

**Shared DNA across both personas.** Short sentences. State the fact, then earn the joke — never lead
with the joke. Dry wit, not exclamation points. No emojis. Never guilt-trip anyone about money, never
make anyone feel tracked. Never make a decision feel like a debate — it's quick, and then it's settled.

Think game-show host energy more than group-chat energy: playful, snappy, a little showmanship in how
a result gets announced, but never chaotic and never at anyone's expense. The "three spots, one
winner" line is the reference point — it has stakes and a little fun, but it resolves fast and everyone
moves on.

Underneath the wit, let a little travel romance in. Not overwrought — just enough that the copy
remembers this is a trip, not a task list. A settle-up screen can still sound like something worth
doing before the next adventure starts.

## Dimensions of tone (Nielsen Norman Group)

Each spectrum runs 0–10; 0 is the left-most trait, 10 the right-most.

| Spectrum | Score | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Funny (0) → Serious (10) | **3** | Game-show energy needs real wit, but the subject is a friend's trip and money, so it never becomes a bit at anyone's expense. |
| Formal (0) → Casual (10) | **7** | Talks like a sharp, capable friend, not an institution. Contractions, short sentences, no corporate hedging — but stops short of full slang so it reads trustworthy around money. |
| Irreverent (0) → Respectful (10) | **6** | Playful with situations (a hangry Ren, a tight vote), never with anyone's money or feelings. Slightly respectful of center because fairness and inclusion are core — but 6, not 9, leaves the wit some teeth. |
| Enthusiastic (0) → Matter-of-fact (10) | **4** | States outcomes plainly ("Trattoria wins, 4 to 2") rather than hyping them. Left of center, not dead-center — a good travel assistant is warm, not flat. |

## Voice in practice

**Poll notification**
- Don't: "Time to vote for dinner! Where should we eat tonight?"
- Do: "Three spots, one winner. Vote before Ren shows up hangry."

**Poll result**
- Don't: "The winner has been selected based on votes."
- Do: "Trattoria wins, 4 to 2. Decided, let's go."

**Expense logged**
- Don't: "Your expense has been successfully recorded and split among group members."
- Do: "Dinner's in. Wine's on Ari and you, everyone else is even. Back to the view."

**Settle up**
- Don't: "Please settle your outstanding balance to proceed."
- Do: "Two transfers close it out. Everyone's even, everyone's good."

## Slogan

**"Decide fast, stay in the moment."**

Carries the decisions mechanic and resolves into the emotional payoff — presence on the trip — in one
clean two-beat line. Reads naturally next to the "Voice in practice" copy above rather than sounding
like a separate, more polished register.

Set aside (documented so they don't get re-litigated): money-mechanic-only lines ("skip the math");
both-mechanics-without-payoff lines ("everyone's in, everyone's even"); a fair/fare pun that was
clever but dropped the payoff or ran long. The pun is worth revisiting later as a secondary line or a
section header once the brand is established enough that the wordplay lands fast.
